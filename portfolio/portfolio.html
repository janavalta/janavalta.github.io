Week 02
Ulises Carrión - Mail Art and the Big Monster from Second Thoughts
Mail Art is more than a decorated message and has two sub-systems. One system is the physical forms that make up the message's content, and the second is the Post System, which the artist cannot control and has its restrictions. Each system cannot exist without the other. Additionally, Mail Art can be judged and create "harmony" if it receives a response. However, it is difficult to measure Mail Art if it is not meant to collect an answer.
Questions:
  • Based on the quote "But, as we have seen, an artist does not become a Mail artist by sending a postcard, no matter how 'beautiful' this card may be," what physical aspects draw the line between a decorated postcard/letter/mail from Mail Art?
  • Given that Mail Art exists, does Email Art exist and what would be the features to distinguish it?
Week 03
Laurel Schwulst & Édouard U - Selections from Creative Independent and Arena How Do You Use the Internet Mindfully
I find it interesting how the reading describes the different ways of creating a website through various nature metaphors that include levels of commitment and maintenance to the website's creation. The first selection also mentions how artists have endless possibilities for designing websites that do not need to follow how big corporations create them. To relate the first and second selections, artists can create website formats that allow visitors to make connections between multiple forms of media that do not inherently relate to each other, making the web a collective experience rather than an isolated one.
Questions:
  • Édouard U includes a section of Lifelong Kindergarten by Mitchel Resnick. Based on the last sentence, what are examples of websites that are "closed-started" while remaining open-ended?
  • In instances when artists create websites as a way to show their portfolio to companies, what are ways for them to design in a way that is "digestible" to employers while "complementing what they do rather than competing or repeating"?
Week 04
Olia Lialina - STILL THERE Ruins and Templates of Geocities from Lost and Living (in) Archives
Olia Lialina describes an interesting perspective of how the archive of Geocities can create a more in-depth understanding of the web and its visual features, such as themed neighborhoods, small images and GIFs, and bordered backgrounds, that were prevalent in the 20th century. She also notes two common forms of announcements on the pages: one being the site developer promising to update the page, and the second being an announcement that the page will no longer update or will move. I also found it compelling and agreed that websites can be “alive” despite not being up-to-date, and I think that is because of how the themed neighborhoods did a great job on emulating a real life neighborhood and community.
Questions:
  • Are there any modern communities reminiscent of Geocities?
  • Similarly, are there aspects of the modern internet, similar to the c.gif, that can be traced back specifically to Geocities?
Week 06
Bojana Coklyat & Shannon Finnegan - Selections from Alt-Text as Poetry Workbook
The reading emphasizes how alt-text can be part of the artwork rather than an afterthought or add-on. The authors also point out how artists can have the power to prioritize the accessibility of their artwork on an institutional level and make tangible change. It was interesting that Coklyat and Finnegan asked us to think of alt-text as poetry and translation, as I have never considered alt-text as another form of different media. I also appreciate how the text brings up how to navigate objectivity and subjectivity in alt-text to prioritize comprehension, conciseness, and creativity.
Questions:
  • What led Coklyat & Finnegan to compare alt-text specifically with poetry and translation, and what other comparisons could be made with alt-text and other forms of media?
  • The authors describe objectivity as something that "validates the perspective of white, cis, middle and upper-class men while marking other perspectives as "subjective," so I wonder how could this be explained further in connection with alt-text and audiences that are not part of those categories?
Week 08
"A Personal Computer for Children of All Cultures" from Decolonising the Digital
Computer programming and design heavily rely on English and the Latin alphabet, which excludes many non-English speakers and reflects a lack of diversity in the industry. Nasser attempts to address this issue by creating a non-English programming language using Arabic. However, the effort cannot significantly change the industry since simply translating existing systems to accommodate other languages does not address the root issue of English's centrality in computer languages. Additionally, this solution places non-English languages as an afterthought in computer science.
Questions:
  • What are some current design practices that try to address this linguistic gap?
  • How can existing internet protocols and computer programming languages be updated to acknowledge this bias?
Week 09
Ben Duvall - Selections from New Modernism(s)
The reading defines graphic design as being composed of surfaces and signs in the form of images and types. Duvall differentiates Modernism, Postmodernism, and Hypermodernism using this definition. Modernism was concerned with communicating the message in an over-simplified way, while Postmodernism rejected existing design systems using "ambiguous" typography.
Questions:
  • What are the limitations of having a hyperlinked sign?
  • In what ways is design at this time evolving past the hyperlinked sign, if any?
Week 11
Dot Tuer - Beyond the New Media Frame The Poetics of Absence in Vera Frenkel’s String Games
Tuer discusses the ideas of absence and connection in Vera Frenkel's artwork, particularly in String Games: Improvisations for Inter-City Video. The text also brings in discussions from other figures about "String Games," one of them being how the artwork demonstrates a significant use of new technology but has not received critical recognition until later. Tuer also mentions a comparison between String Games and the Body Missing website and while both have different purposes, they show a "pioneering use of technology."
Questions:
  • How do modern technologies demonstrate this idea of "poetics of absence"?
  • Why was Cat's Cradle used to model the String Games?
Week 13
Neta Bomani & Sabii Borno - Beyond Dark Matter from Logic Issue 15 Beacons
“Beyond Dark Matter” tells an important story reflecting the biases and problematic histories within technology. Gem learns about these numerous events that are often untold through the installation of The Way. She learns to combat the limitations of technology with The Beyond by finding solutions that are community-based.
Questions:
  • In what ways are modern technologies still reflecting these biases and problematic histories?
  • In turn, what are current community-based solutions to these problems?
Week 14
Boris Groys - Art on the Internet
"Art on the Internet" delves into how the Internet has changed art and its essence. Although I found parts of the essay difficult to follow, his discussion on the de-fictionalization and fictionalization of art was captivating. Groys argues that the Internet has shifted art towards a more interactive and communicative process, moving away from traditional object-based forms. He also explores the differences between art documentation in real life and its online presence, emphasizing how the creative process often happens in isolation. This explains why artists often prefer working in private spaces rather than in front of an audience. Furthermore, Groys raises an intriguing argument regarding the rejection of identification in this scenario, indicating an intricate relationship between artistic expression, digital representation, and hidden identity.
Questions:
  • The reading talks about how art and politics share a space in the present and want to shape the future, but they differ in how present they are in the future. In this case, where would political art fall?
  • Groys notes that "everything disappears rather than emerges - most Internet productions (and personae) never get the degree of public attention that their authors hoped to achieve." Are there current practices that combat this?
